# Matt’s Story and Answer to Questions

### Why I started Typespace

I’m driving home from work after another decent day at my perfect tech job.

Absolutely nothing out of the ordinary.

Except this average afternoon - that is exactly my signal.

I’m the frog being boiled alive. 

I’m accepting mild-discontent.

I’m accepting reorganizing my to-dos because I don’t know what to do.

I’m accepting spending ages after-hours making social media content but having no direction.

I’m accepting average.

I have to jump. 

Now.

I’ve known for ages that I dream of starting my own business.
Just a dream.

I know that I want to build something grand that flowers into a spectacle that helps and inspires thousands of people.

Just more romance.

But now that I know I have to jump - I start taking my ideas more seriously.

I start with idea number 1. The idea I’ve been mulling over for almost a year.

I open my scrapbook and start getting into the nitty-gritty details of what needs to be built:

Firstly, you should know that I’m a die-hard personal brand enthusiast.

I just made that term up right now - I will not be diving into what that means but you get the idea.

I just love the idea that the internet has given individuals the ability to grow a massive community, help thousands of people and grow businesses.

People follow people. 

While sometimes the algorithms get on my nerves, I’m still a fanboy to so many creators.

They have taught me to earn my first money online (114x my monthly allowance at 16 - 1 month of animating from online tutorials), and taught me how to literally take control of my own body (I’ve watched every single calisthenics video on Youtube ever). 

They also taught me how to videography & DaVinci Resolve - [see my Youtube!](https://www.youtube.com/@itsderman?sub_confirmation=1).

P.S. If you want to get started in calisthenics but don’t care for skills - [@Kboges](https://www.youtube.com/@Kboges/) is just legendarily simple and to the point. 

The point is - that my idea sprung out of the problems I experienced - being the market itself!

Back to the idea 👇.

I’ve been thinking about an app that systematizes content redistribution (initially I wanted to call it redistribute.ai or something).

When I like a song - I usually get pretty obsessed.
That song is on repeat. and repeat.

(my poor, poor headphones, especially since I’m a techno-head).

When I like a post, a thread, a video, a reel. It’s usually not a new idea.

It’s very, very rare that it’s a new idea.

And when it is, the “idea mastermind!” would be losing out by not just writing about that idea again and putting it out into the world.

Musicians publish a song once for you to listen on repeat.

Personal brands have to publish that same “song” over and over for you to see it.

You keep your brand top-of-mind by repackaging the same ideas over and over.

And I’m constantly surprised at how I love those same idea, by the same people, every single bloody time.

And I’m constantly surprised at how long it takes me to write and film even half of what my “competitors” are making. 

So I want to make a system that does that nearly on autopilot, but still with enough creative control for nerds like me.

I want us solopreneurs to stand a chance against massive social media teams that the giants have at their disposal.

Here are the ideas it started around:

- X threads = LinkedIn carousels = Instagram carousels = Threads etc. etc. I should be able to write out a thread once, and have it designed and auto-published everywhere.
- The post does well, or I tag it and it’s added to my “Guiding Lights” as I call them (posts that my audience enjoys/resonate with).
- In 2 months time, I see that published post resurface in my dashboard - I use AI to change the post - say e.g. changing the pain-point that I talk about in relation to the idea.
- One-click it’s added to queue again!
- Now I can get back to building the real product/delivering a service to my community!

Boy oh boy I did not know how much more work it would entail 😂.

I’m incredibly proud of what I’ve built now- I use it every time I post on LinkedIn.

I’ve decided I just love LinkedIn in the most, and after spending MONTHS on the X thread/X post AI I barely use it.

#LinkedInIsLife

But really, I just have to set my focus on one core product and audience at first.

It’s too difficult to build AND build an audience on multiple platforms.

If you want to use it just for X it is actually really really good - I just barely talk about it.

Now I’m building a LinkedIn app that has it’s first 80 users! It does idea finding, inspiration, writing, scheduling, carousels and much more! Analytics and commenting is coming soon!

Background & Journey

***“What’s one childhood or early life experience that shaped the way you work today?”***

When I was 16 my favourite Youtuber released a $2000 competition for his Youtube intro - a 3-8s animation.

I knew absolutely nothing about animation or 3d modelling or video editing.

But I couldn’t sleep at night - I was drawing frames, ideas and writing ideas. I had a vision of what I wanted to create.

Then I realized that actually - I (the complete noob) had the best chance of winning.

I had the school holidays coming up, and I could spend all day, every day learning and animating. The real animation artists wouldn’t have that luxury.

I decided to give it my shot. 

I didn’t leave my computer for weeks. 

It was tough, I doubted myself a lot and hit my keyboard a good few times.

My grandparents thought I was crazy.

But I knew I would win.
And I did.

That taught me an amazing lesson in the power in believing in yourself.

I had utter conviction in my success, despite any proof of my ability.
And that was the reason I won.

Now I’m applying that belief, the conviction, and the complete focus on my goal to my LinkedIn growth app.

Same Conviction

Same Focus
Much longer time horizon

***“When you first started your journey as a founder, what did life look like for you?”***

I was working blind on what I thought were the most important things, with no paying customers and running on savings. Believing I could do it.

***“What fears did you secretly carry in the beginning?”***

I never doubted that I wouldn’t succeed - not for a second.

I was building something that would guarantee my future would be brighter on the other side of it, no matter the outcome.

- not enough paying customers? Use the app as proof of abilities to land higher paying tech job, or land a new client
- Lots of customers and I get to shift into an industry I’m much more passionate about (the creator economy)

I fear being “average” and not taking any risks to doing something bigger, innovative and using the extent of my abilities. I fear just taking life as it comes and not really trying to do anything in particular.

I also feared that it would hurt my relation with my partner, friends and family along the way. But I knew that the quality of the (amazing) people around would mean they would understand if I said no to things. 

***Struggles & Turning Points***

***“What was the hardest season of your journey and how did you push through?”***

The hardest season was this last winter before my first paying users.

- Felt trapped physically in our tiny cold apartment and with the business
- It felt like every day I stumbled across another competitor app that was ahead of me in some way. Crushing
- I struggled prioritizing and worked erratically between tasks
- Then I would have one day of churning out a lot of code, and it would just give me more anxiety at all the previous days that I didn’t have that level of output.

I kept on telling myself I had bigger long-term goals and a grander vision for what I was building that would make it shine above everything else in the long run.

I’ve always been a builder.

When it’s time to build something I become obsessed (and a bit too perfectionistic most of the time) 

I told myself this was just the last push before I started seeing results trickle in, and they slowly did.

***“Was there a moment you felt like giving up? What kept you going?”***

This winter, I thought about giving up and going back to my job full-time at least about 5 times a day 😂.

***“Who or what gave you strength when things got really tough?”***

In superhero stories, the hero goes through his trials and tribulations before overcoming them and winning! 

Real life is much more sinister.

In real life, your life slowly gets worse. Your environment, finances, mind and body slowly succumb to entropy - chaos.

When you realize that’s happening - you must be on full alert.

When I’m sad, I:

- Go do exercise. This is the easiest fix. Super simple. Gym + music = win.
- Remind myself to be grateful - of my partner, family, friends, community, skills, food in the fridge, roof over my head, beautiful sunsets. I go hug a tree or walk on the mountain barefoot. simple stuff.
- Remind myself that desiring a positive experience is itself a negative experience, and the acceptance of a negative experience is itself a positive experience.

But when I realize my life is creeping slowly into disarray, I :

- Remind myself that the thing I’m procrastinating about is the thing that needs to be done the most in order to make me feel better.
- Remind myself that emotions are your subjective reaction to perception. In theory, I can simply operationalize how I respond to them.
- Sometimes I imagine this as if I was super angry at something, and then I just suddenly started laughing. The forced laughter would be contagious, and within about 15s I probably wouldn’t be angry. I can do the same type of thing with any emotion. If I want to. Sometimes, it is good to sit and experience your emotions.

***Personal Side***

***“How has marriage/family shaped the way you lead and make decisions?”***

My dad has shaped my confidence and commitment to building this app. He’s a software guru and has given me opportunity and taught me so much! I’ve been so lucky to work with him in his business - I’m super grateful for the time I get to work with him!

My partner has made my goals less self-centred - I always keep her in mind with every large decision. This typically means less gung-ho risky decisions. Before meeting her I would say I’d risk absolutely everything over and over and live in a shack (with wifi) until I made it. That type of game plan is no longer on the cards for instance 😂.

***“What sacrifices did you or your family make for this journey?”***

My partner had to accept the uncertainty of me starting this business, and it made her feel less safe without my regular income, even though our standard of living didn’t change at all. This has been hard for us, but she knows that it will be fine.

I promise that I will be the man and care for her. I enjoy the responsibility.

***“How do you balance ambition with personal relationships?”***

If I can’t build my business and also keep a relationship/see friends - then how can single moms run a business and also look after 4 kids. 

If I need to spend less time socializing in a given week - it’s up to me to practice the mental discipline of saying no.

Saying no is really hard a lot of the time. The more you do it though, the more the sting gets easier to deal with.

***Transformation & Lessons***

***“What’s the biggest change you’ve noticed in yourself over the years?”***

I’ve become a lot better at assessing software/architecting software from a high level. I can much better assess the scope of features beforehand.

I’ve learnt that everyone has a story to tell.

I’m a lot more aware of the vague, naive attitude I had to entrepreneurship before I started really trying to build a specific app. I used to hide in the shadows of learning and absorbing information and planning and drawing and dreaming. I had beautiful vague dreams that didn’t involve solving any real problems in the world.

I’ve learn that art and creation for its own sake must remain a separate part of my life.

It’s much better that the path you take to put food on the table is not romanticized at all. Weight the odds, choose the risks you’re willing to take to earn the most amount of money you can. What you do with your time later in life, or free time, is a different story.

***“Looking back, what do you wish your younger self knew?”***

1. I wish I knew that you’ve got to be SPECIFIC.

One Solution. One Person. One Service (or product).

A real business isn’t some vague beautiful idea. It solves a real fking problem

Now go and knock on people’s doors, phone, message DM until it has some money flowing.

Then start thinking about content and building what you really want it to be.

Much less stressful building that way.

1. Stop planning. Start doing. Remember how it used to look when I did painting. Eisel, paints, water, newspaper absolute mess all around the room. paper, books, discarded drafts. But when I stepped in to paint, put my earphones in and apron on, shit got done!
2. Set an alarm in the morning, and drink black coffee/fast until 12ish. That’s the only routine you ever need to think about. Period.
3. Own your circumstance, and recognize how many wasted thoughts are spent on blaming and thinking about situations that I can’t exert force on to change. Something can always be done, and it always always starts with yourself.
4. I also wish I knew what hard work actually means. I still haven’t quite figured it out.
    - It means concentrating and doing complicated difficult work when you’re tired. When you’re distracted or feeling FOMO.
    - It means actually doing work that will produce real results, even if there’s lots of other “work” that you could do that will actually just waste time. That other works could make my OCD perfectionist brain happy, but be nearly useless.
    - It means saying no to things when the only person in the entire world who is going to see the benefits of you saying no is you. Everyone else will not.
    - It means working when you’re tired, and then still working the same tomorrow.
    - It means consistency, with bursts/sprints as often as you can.

***“What’s one principle you live by now that you didn’t value before?”***

I use to not care for a good coffee - instant, burnt, capuccino sachets ehhh

Now I only drink the finest espressos.

(just kidding).

Simplicity.

I used to think things were so much more complicated than they are.
They’re not. What matters can always be boiled down to incredible simplicity.

The nuance might take centuries to really understand anyway, so focus on the simple boring stuff.

***Future & Inspiration***

A massive source of inspiration to me has been the image of myself as a father, and the lessons and fun and inspiration and pride I could place in my kids. 

So besides just being remembered as a great son/husband/dad/friend etc. I’d like people to remember me as the guy that quietly went and built a world (apps, writing, art, and films) that inspired and enabled human agency, and served them (the community) to be creative and intentional. 

P.S. right now I’m focusing on the apps and writing part in it’s basic form - but I do have a Youtube channel I focused on last year which you can find on [matterman.com](http://matterman.com) lol

Choose a specific problem you’re going to solve for a business or person (that isn’t B2C) and go for it. Your life will eventually reflect what you believe in your mind.

***“What keeps you excited for the future, despite challenges?”***

I just love building and creating things! No matter what happens in this world, I believe there will always be space and opportunity for me to be economically and socially useful by building stuff so I’ll be fine. Yay cool!

Also, I’m inspired by my favourite artist too! Writers, musicians, developers, painters!

# Story - Long dump

Lots of people ask me why I'm building a LinkedIn marketing app.

How are you going to compete with big players? What makes it different?

Here's a story of how I got here & how I plan to make it different (a timeline):

Dec 2023:

Finish my degree and start work at an awesome tech job.

2024:

Desperate to spend my free time building my future, but I have no clue what. I see what everyone else sees. People from all over the world building businesses through their personal brands on social media.

I also see loads of other opportunities literally everywhere but I just want to stick to one thing so that I can get really good at it, and it has the highest likelihood of equipping me for whatever I might pivot to in the future.

So I decide to learn as much as I can about marketing, psychology, copywriting etc. etc. and spent 1000s on courses.

Nov 2024:

Realize I'm being an idiot and that real learning comes from applying - building - so I'm not going to learn anything if I don't apply it to something specific.

I chose to build a Taplio killer because:

I used to lovvvee writing in high school (and it's more or less how I got in in the first place), and now my passion was starting to re-kindle as I saw the art that the likes of Kieran Drew and Shaan Puri were writing on X.

(I thought LinkedIn was mostly corporate cringe at the time).

But I also lovvvvee building things. That's my real passion.

All this time, I'd been trying out all the different social media writing and scheduling tools in the process of learning - and had since formed loads of ideas in my head about all the gripes I and others were having, and what I wanted to see in a similar tool that could help me build my personal brand.

I didn't yet have an audience to ask these questions to, so I started off on a COMPLETELY different trajectory what Typespace is today.

(I wanted to build a tool for YOUTUBERS to help them starting building their brand on X/LinkedIn/Threads/Insta - see my youtube channel for how that started (I learnt a ton, but that's not where I'm focused now).

By Dec I decided to go full-time on building what I called Typeway, since I had enough experience as a dev contractor to still do great work when the need arose.

June 2025:

Skip ahead a few months, and I'm really feeling the f**king struggle.

- I had to rebuild MANY MANY features in multiple different ways to find the ways that work best. It felt like I waste of time, but I tell myself my competitors would have to do the same thing too.
- I still had no customers because I was spending my time building the app, getting frustrated at things breaking and not being to the standard that I wanted.

Aug 2025:

Things still aren't they way I want - so I decide to say F**k it and just start having fun on LinkedIn anyway.

Rest of the story in first comment underneath me staring into my bright future.

So yes, I discovered I actually enjoy this weird LinkedIn game. It's fun.

By now I've got the core inspo, post generation and scheduling features nailed in the app - confidently better than anything else I've tried.

There's still so much I want to build to make it clearly stand out from amongst the other (boring) LinkedIn tools, but that needs to wait.

Clearly, one the main legs I'm standing on is that this is not some monolith company you're engaging with. You signup, I mail you, we have a call if you want, it's personal. I'm the brand and the builder. You want a feature, you simply ask (nicely).

I'm not building some cheap tool. This is premium product that I want to be the Notion of LinkedIn.

Anyway, enough of that. For now I'm just spending my time engaging and chatting to people I think I can help (or just have a good time learning from and getting to know).
